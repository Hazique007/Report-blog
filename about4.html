Abstract


Hydrogen has surfaced as a viable solution to the global energy and environmental problems in an era characterized by urgent worries about climate change and the necessity of moving away from fossil fuels and hence providing sustainable alternative(s). The purpose of this study article is to thoroughly investigate hydrogen's potential as the fuel of the future. This paper will explore the production of energy by the hydrogen fuel in two main ways of production, hydrogen fuel cell and hydrogen combustion. Considering both the financial and environmental viability of it. We hope that this thorough analysis will add to the continuing conversation on the critical role hydrogen may play in our energy transition.


Introduction


The main features of a fuel cell are shown in Figure1, and Figure 2 shows the working of a fuel cell. The figure includes a fuel electrode on anode, air electrode on cathode as oxidant and an electrolyte. The fuel and oxidizing agents are fed through the electrodes and are brought into contact with the electrolyte. Reaction between the oxidizing agent and fuel produces voltage at the electrodes. The positive terminal of the cell is the oxygen electrode, and the negative terminal of the battery is the fuel. The electrons with other ions are conducted through the electrolyte and travels from one electrode to another, completing the electrical circuit to satisfy the connected external load. The fuel cell continues to operate as the current flows through the load and the reactants (fuel and oxidant) are fed. The electromotive force (emf) of the fuel cell is in the order of 1V. In series, many cells are connected to provide a terminal voltage which is known as ‘module.' Depending on the mode of operation, a fuel cell battery constitutes several modules connected either in series or parallel.

The Gibbs free energy change, accompanying the oxidation of the fuel at the absolute temperature relates to the corresponding enthalpy and entropy changes given in equation 1.

The ∆G determines the maximum emf of the cell in terms of equation 2.



The basics of fuel cells can be explained using the hydrogen-oxygen cell. Two electrodes consisting of platinum foil submerged in an electrolyte (potassium hydroxide or sulfuric acid solution). Hydrogen gas is delivered into the fuel cell's anode, where it dissociates in the presence of a catalyst to generate H+ ions and electrons before moving on to the electrolyte. Oxygen gas is introduced to the cathode, where it picks up electrons and combines with the water in the electrolyte (potassium hydroxide KOH solution) to produce hydroxyl ions (OH-). The OH- and H+ ions in the electrolyte are in balance with the water (H2O). A DC power of 100 to 200 mA/cm2 is applied in at a voltage of roughly 1V, usually on the electrical output of a single cell. Equation 3 states that the hydrogen electrode takes the place of the total reactions of hydrogen ions consumed at the oxygen electrode.

H2 + ½ O2 → H2O

In Hydrogen Combustion, Hydrogen (H2) is burned (combustion i.e. reaction with oxygen) which due to the combustion reaction gives out thermal energy as is given in combustion engines, in this reaction a mixture of H2 and air is combusted in a combustion chamber either with compression ignition method or spark ignition, the latter is more preferred as hydrogen has very high self-combustion temperature which requires very high compression ratios, whereas the ignition energy is very low so it makes it easily combustible by spark ignition method, this thermal energy obtained in combustion is further converted to a suitable form of energy preferably mechanical like in automobile engines the energy output reaction can simply be given as :

2H2 + O2 → 2H2O

It does also have its own benefits such as it is clean burning, the primary combustion product is water without CO2, but some NOx is there in hydrogen combustion

Combustive properties of Hydrogen


The following properties of Hydrogen contribute to its use as a combustible fuel:

5.1 Wide range of Flammability:

Hydrogen possesses a broader flammability range compared to other fuels, allowing it to be burned in an internal combustion engine across a wide spectrum of fuel-air mixtures. One notable benefit is hydrogen's ability to operate on a lean mixture, which contains less fuel than the theoretical, stoichiometric, or chemically ideal amount required for combustion with a given amount of air. This characteristic facilitates the ease of starting an engine on hydrogen. Typically, running a vehicle on a lean mixture enhances fuel economy and results in a more complete combustion reaction. Moreover, the final combustion temperature tends to be lower, which diminishes the emission of pollutants like nitrogen oxides in the exhaust. However, there's a threshold to the leanness of the engine's operation, as excessively lean mixtures can markedly decrease power output by reducing the volumetric heating value of the air/fuel mixture.

5.2 Small Quenching Distance:

Hydrogen possesses a shorter quenching distance compared to gasoline, which means hydrogen flames travel nearer to the cylinder walls before extinguishing. As a result, quenching a hydrogen flame is more challenging than a gasoline flame. This reduced quenching distance may also heighten the risk of backfire, as flames from a hydrogen-air mixture can pass through a nearly closed intake valve more easily than those from a hydrocarbon-air mixture.

5.3 High Auto-ignition temperature:

Hydrogen possesses a relatively high autoignition temperature, which significantly impacts the compression of a hydrogen-air mixture. The autoignition temperature is a crucial determinant of the feasible compression ratio for an engine, as the temperature increase during compression correlates with this ratio. Exceeding hydrogen's autoignition temperature can lead to premature ignition, thereby capping the compression ratio. Hydrogen's elevated autoignition temperature permits the use of higher compression ratios in hydrogen engines compared to hydrocarbon engines, which is beneficial for thermal efficiency. However, igniting hydrogen in a compression ignition or diesel engine is challenging due to the high temperatures required for ignition. The autoignition temperature, the lowest temperature needed to start self-sustained combustion in a fuel mixture without external ignition, is notably high for hydrogen at 585ºC. This makes igniting a hydrogen-air mixture with heat alone difficult, necessitating an additional ignition source.

5.4 High Flame Speed:

Hydrogen exhibits a high flame speed at stoichiometric ratios. In such conditions, its flame speed is almost an order of magnitude greater than gasoline's, allowing hydrogen engines to more closely mimic the ideal thermodynamic cycle. However, at leaner mixtures, the flame speed significantly reduces. Both flame velocity and adiabatic flame temperature are crucial for engine operation and control, affecting thermal efficiency, combustion stability, and emissions. The relationship between laminar flame velocity and flame temperature can be graphically represented as a function of the equivalence ratio.

5.5 High Diffusivity:

Hydrogen possesses a high diffusivity, allowing it to disperse in the air much more effectively than gasoline. This characteristic is beneficial for two primary reasons. First, it aids in creating a uniform mixture of fuel and air. Second, in the event of a hydrogen leak, the gas disperses quickly, which can prevent or reduce the risk of unsafe conditions.

5.6 Low Density:

Hydrogen's low density presents two significant challenges for its use in internal combustion engines. First, a substantial volume is required to store sufficient hydrogen for an acceptable driving range. Second, the energy density of a hydrogen-air mixture is lower, which diminishes the power output.

5.7 Minimum ignition source energy:

The minimum ignition source energy is the lowest energy needed to ignite a fuel-air mixture with an ignition source, such as a spark discharge. For a hydrogen air mixture, it is 0.02 mJ, in contrast to 0.24 mJ for a petrol-air mixture, and remains roughly constant across the flammability range. The hydrogen-air mix's low minimum ignition energy signifies that a much weaker spark is sufficient for ignition. Consequently, combustion can be initiated with a basic glow plug or resistance hot-wire, ensuring quick ignition of the charge in the combustion chamber.




Hydrogen used in Internal Combustion Engine


6.1 Hydrogen use in diesel engines: Utilizing hydrogen as a supplementary fuel alongside diesel in internal combustion (IC) compression ignition (CI) engines offers several benefits. Primarily, it enhances the hydrogen-to-carbon (H/C) ratio of the fuel mix. Additionally, the introduction of small quantities of hydrogen into a diesel engine can reduce the heterogeneity of the diesel fuel spray. This is due to hydrogen's high diffusivity, which promotes a more homogenous and well premixed combustible mixture with air. Consequently, this can potentially eliminate the formation of hydrocarbons, carbon monoxide, and carbon dioxide during combustion, although trace amounts might still be produced from the incomplete combustion of lubricating oil within the combustion chamber. However, hydrogen alone cannot serve as the exclusive fuel in a CI engine because the compression temperature is insufficient to ignite the hydrogen, given its higher self ignition temperature. Therefore, without the aid of a spark plug or glow plug, hydrogen is not a viable sole fuel option for diesel engines.

6.2 Hydrogen use in spark ignition (SI) engines: Hydrogen serves as a viable fuel option for internal combustion engines, akin to spark-ignited gasoline engines. Research on hydrogen (H2) as a fuel predominantly explores its use in spark-ignited engines. Hydrogen presents several advantageous properties for such applications, including low ignition energy, rapid flame propagation speed, and a broad operational range. When hydrogen is mixed with air, it forms a combustible mixture that can be ignited in a conventional spark ignition engine at an equivalence ratio that is below the lean flammability limit of a gasoline-air mixture. This ultra-lean combustion process yields low flame temperatures, resulting in reduced heat transfer to the engine walls, enhanced engine efficiency, and diminished NOx emissions in the exhaust.




Basic difference between Combustion and Fuel Cell Technologies


Hydrogen combustion engines and hydrogen fuel cells are two distinct methods of utilizing hydrogen as a fuel source. Hydrogen combustion engines operate similarly to traditional internal combustion engines, burning hydrogen in a combustion chamber to generate power for the vehicle. On the other hand, hydrogen fuel cells use a chemical reaction between hydrogen and oxygen to produce electricity, which then powers an electric motor.




Comparison between Combustion and Fuel Cell Technologies


8.1 Based on Principle: Hydrogen combustion engines and hydrogen fuel cells are two distinct methods of utilizing hydrogen as a fuel source. Hydrogen combustion engines operate similarly to traditional internal combustion engines, burning hydrogen in a combustion chamber to generate power for the vehicle. On the other hand, hydrogen fuel cells use a chemical reaction between hydrogen and oxygen to produce electricity, which then powers an electric motor.

8.2 Comparison Based on Efficiency: Hydrogen fuel cells generally offer higher efficiency compared to hydrogen combustion engines, with fuel cells achieving efficiency ratings of up to 60%, while combustion engines reach only 20-25%. This higher efficiency makes hydrogen fuel cells more cost-effective to run and more applicable to larger vehicles that require high fuel efficiency for long-distance driving. However, the adoption of hydrogen fuel cell vehicles has been slowed due to a lack of refueling infrastructure and the expensive nature of raw hydrogen gas.

8.3 Sustainability: Hydrogen combustion engines, while less efficient, have the advantage of being relatively simple and easy to manufacture. They also have a longer range than hydrogen fuel cells and are more efficient than gasoline engines. However, they produce some emissions, including nitrogen oxides (NOx), and the infrastructure for refueling hydrogen vehicles is not as well developed as that for gasoline or battery-electric vehicles.

Conclusion


To make a decision, hydrogen fuel cells offer higher efficiency and are more environmentally friendly, but they are more complex and expensive to manufacture. Hydrogen combustion engines, while less efficient and producing emissions, are simpler, easier to manufacture, and have a longer range. The choice between the two technologies depends on the specific needs and goals of the application, as well as the availability and cost of hydrogen refueling infrastructure.

Hydrogen combustion engines, while less efficient, have the advantage of being relatively simple and easy to manufacture. They also have a longer range than hydrogen fuel cells and are more efficient than gasoline engines. However, they produce some emissions, including nitrogen oxides (NOx), and the infrastructure for refueling hydrogen vehicles is not as well developed as that for gasoline or battery-electric vehicles. While hydrogen combustion produces emissions like nitrogen oxides, it is simpler to manufacture and offers a longer range than fuel cells. On the other hand, hydrogen fuel cells are more efficient, producing no emissions, but are complex and expensive to manufacture. Fuel cells require less hydrogen storage, making them more practical, especially for larger vehicles needing high fuel efficiency

Ultimately, the choice between the two technologies depends on factors like efficiency, emissions, cost, and infrastructure availability.
