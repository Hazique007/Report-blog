Role of Modern Technologies in Agriculture

Published on April 17, 2024 by Avinash Kumar, Saadul-Haq, Naman
Department of Civil Engineering , Zakir Husain college of Engineering and Technology, Aligarh Muslim University



Abstract


Modern technologies have transformed the field of agriculture in many ways, enabling farmers to improve efficiency, productivity, and sustainability. Precision farming, automated farming, biotechnology, data analytics, and sustainable agriculture are some of the key areas where modern technologies have had a significant impact. Precision farming technologies such as GPS, drones, and sensors help farmers monitor and analyse their crops, soil, and weather conditions with high precision, leading to optimized crop yields and reduced waste. Automated machinery and robotics increase productivity, reduce labour costs, and minimize environmental impact. Biotechnology has enabled farmers to produce crops that are more resistant to pests, diseases, and adverse environmental conditions, leading to higher crop yields and improved food quality and safety. Data analytics and artificial intelligence algorithms provide valuable insights and predictions about crop growth, yields, and disease outbreaks, helping farmers make informed decisions. Overall, modern technologies have played a crucial role in advancing the field of agriculture, helping farmers increase efficiency, productivity, and sustainability while producing high quality and safe food.

Introduction


Agriculture has been an essential part of human civilization for thousands of years, providing food, fiber, and other resources for human consumption and economic development. Over time, advancements in technology have played a significant role in the evolution of agriculture, from the domestication of plants and animals to the use of mechanized tools and techniques in farming practices. In recent years, modern technologies have revolutionized the field of agriculture, enabling farmers to increase efficiency, productivity, and sustainability while producing high-quality and safe food (Rehman et al., 2016).

According to a report by the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations, the world's population is projected to reach 9.7 billion by 2050, which will increase the demand for food by 70%. This demand can be met only through the adoption of modern technologies in agriculture, which can increase crop yields, reduce waste, and improve food quality and safety. Furthermore, modern technologies can help farmers adapt to the challenges posed by climate change, water scarcity, and soil degradation, which threaten the sustainability of agriculture.

Sensors have become ubiquitous in various fields, from measuring bodily parameters to enabling automated driving. They play a crucial role in detecting and analysing information in modern applications of science, engineering, and technology, particularly those involving computer vision. One area where smart sensors are increasingly employed is the Internet of Things (IoT), which involves wireless networks and distributed sensors that collect and analyse real-time data to produce desired outcomes through appropriate processing.


Modern agricultural technology and machinery used in agriculture


Sensors-

Sensors have become ubiquitous in various fields, from measuring bodily parameters to enabling automated driving. They play a crucial role in detecting and analysing information in modern applications of science, engineering, and technology, particularly those involving computer vision. One area where smart sensors are increasingly employed is the Internet of Things (IoT), which involves wireless networks and distributed sensors that collect and analyse real-time data to produce desired outcomes through appropriate processing. Communications technology has evolved rapidly in India and made smart farming a possibility. Sensors are now being used in agriculture to provide data to farmers to monitor and optimize crops given the environmental conditions and challenges.

In agriculture, we use various types of sensors such as:-

1. Electronic nose sensors:- Electronic nose sensors are becoming increasingly important in agriculture as they offer a fast, reliable, and non-destructive way to detect plant diseases, monitor soil health, and identify insect infestations. By analysing the volatile organic compounds (VOCs) released by plants, electronic noses can provide farmers with valuable information about the health of their crops and soil. In crop disease detection, e-noses can identify the specific VOC profile associated with a diseased plant, allowing for early detection and treatment to prevent the spread of the disease. In soil monitoring, electronic noses can detect changes in the soil VOC profile, which can indicate changes in the soil microbial community, nutrient levels, and overall soil health. This information can help farmers to adjust their farming practices to optimize crop yields and minimize the use of fertilizers and pesticides.

2. Electrochemical sensors:- Electrochemical sensors are increasingly being used in agriculture for a range of applications, including soil monitoring, plant health assessment, and food quality control. These sensors work by detecting and measuring chemical compounds and reactions using electrochemical methods. In soil monitoring, electrochemical sensors can measure parameters such as soil moisture, pH, and nutrient levels, allowing farmers to optimize irrigation and fertilization. These sensors can also detect the presence of pollutants in the soil, such as heavy metals, pesticides, and herbicides, which can have harmful effects on plant growth and human health.




Drones:


Drones, also known as unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs), are increasingly being used in agriculture for a variety of purposes. They can be equipped with various sensors and cameras to capture images and data from crops, soil, and livestock. One of the primary uses of drones in agriculture is for crop monitoring and mapping. Drones equipped with cameras and sensors can provide high-resolution images and data on crop health, growth rates, and nutrient levels. This information can be used by farmers to optimize their crop management practices, reduce input costs, and increase yields (Ahirwar et al., 2019). Applications of Drone-

• Soil and field analysis
• Irrigation
• Crop Spraying
• Health Assessment.


GPS:


Global Positioning System (GPS) has revolutionized the agriculture industry by providing accurate and real-time information for precision farming. With GPS technology, farmers can map their fields, track equipment, monitor crop growth, and optimize yield. GPS can help farmers create precise maps of their fields, allowing them to identify areas that require different treatments, such as fertilizers or pesticides. By using GPS data, farmers can optimize their use of resources, reduce waste, and improve crop yields. In addition, GPS technology can help farmers track their equipment, such as tractors and harvesters, in real-time. This allows them to better manage their equipment, reduce downtime, and improve efficiency. GPS can also be used to monitor crop growth and predict yields. By collecting data on soil moisture, temperature, and other environmental factors, farmers can optimize irrigation and fertilization schedules, resulting in healthier plants and better yields (Yousefi and Razdari, 2015).


Smartphones:


Mobile technology has also been playing a significant role in monitoring and controlling crop irrigation systems. With this modern technology, farmers can control their irrigation systems via smartphones and computers instead of driving to each field.
Moisture sensors planted underground can provide information regarding the moisture levels present at certain depths in the soil.


Biotechnology:


Biotechnology in agriculture refers to the application of scientific techniques to modify and improve plants, animals, and microorganisms for agricultural purposes. This can include genetic engineering to create crops with desirable traits, such as resistance to pests or tolerance to drought, or the development of vaccines or other treatments for livestock diseases. Biotechnology can also involve the use of microorganisms to improve soil fertility or control plant diseases.


Automation and Robotics:


The agricultural sector is experiencing a shift towards automation, with a growing emphasis on autonomous processes such as planting, inspection, pruning, spraying, and harvesting. This shift is aimed at reducing the labour-intensive nature of traditional farming practices. Automated agriculture encompasses any machineries and robots that is designed to replace manual intervention in agricultural operations. The focus of agricultural automation is primarily on autonomous vehicles such as tractors or robots, which aim to minimize the challenging, dangerous, and time consuming working conditions encountered by farmers. Automation also provides precise and efficient operation and control systems while maintaining high output quality and quantity that meet human consumption standards.




Machine Learning:


Machine learning algorithms are transforming agriculture by predicting crop yields based on weather and soil data, enabling informed decisions. These algorithms detect plant diseases and pest infestations through image processing, aiding timely interventions. ML also assesses soil health by analyzing nutrient levels and recommending management practices, promoting sustainability.




Conclusion:


Modern technologies have significantly impacted the agricultural industry, providing new opportunities for increased efficiency, sustainability, and productivity. With the help of automation and robotics, farmers can accomplish their tasks with greater precision, speed, and accuracy, while reducing labour costs and risks. Biotechnology, precision farming, and data analytics have also played a vital role in shaping modern agriculture, enabling farmers to optimize their production by enhancing crop yields, improving disease and pest management, and reducing waste. Cloud computing and IoT have provided farmers with easy access to real-time data, enabling them to make better decisions, while reducing costs and increasing profitability.

Overall, the integration of modern technologies in agriculture has helped to address many of the challenges facing the industry, and it is expected to continue to drive innovation and growth in the future.

The world population is slated to grow to about 9 billion by 2050. The challenge is to find ways and means to produce enough to feed it. The challenge of reducing acreage under agriculture and food wastage in production and distribution is having a major impact on the world. The increasing role of technology in agriculture to address these issues is the only way forward to a food-secure future. Technology can help save foreign exchange for countries, increase productivity, and lead to an improvement in the overall standard of farmer communities. India has a long way to go in the adoption of modern farming practices through technology. The pace is slow and path-breaking efforts need to be made to educate farmers about the benefits to be had with technology. Transcending the barriers of archaic farming practices and medieval mindsets is a challenge that needs to be overcome for a better tomorrow. Technology in agriculture has the potential to truly lead India to be “Atmanirbhar Bharat” in all respects, and be less dependent on extraneous factors.
